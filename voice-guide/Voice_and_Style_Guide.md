# Fluid / We-Commerce Voice & Style Guide

## Overview

This guide defines how Fluid communicates — the tone, the structure, the words we use, and the patterns we avoid. It exists so that any piece of content created for Fluid, whether a homepage headline, a feature explainer, a social post, or a sales deck, feels like it came from the same place.

The shorthand reference for this voice is **"Brian Chesky style"** — named for how the Airbnb CEO communicates: direct, grounded in specific moments, confident without being arrogant, human without being informal. He doesn't explain what a feature does — he explains what it means. He doesn't list benefits — he tells you what was broken and how it got fixed.

Use this guide alongside the Fluid knowledge base files. Those files tell you *what* is true about Fluid. This file tells you *how to say it*.

---

## The Core Voice

### Short sentences. Declarative statements. No hedging.

The Fluid voice is confident. It does not qualify. It does not say "we believe we can help you" — it says "we built this to fix that." It does not say "solutions" or "offerings" — it says what the thing actually does.

When in doubt, make the sentence shorter.

**Wrong:**
> Fluid offers a comprehensive suite of tools designed to help direct selling companies manage the complex attribution requirements unique to their industry.

**Right:**
> Direct selling has an attribution problem. Reps don't trust the system. So they stop sharing. Fluid fixes that.

---

### Ground everything in a specific moment or real person.

Abstract claims die. Specific moments live. The most effective Fluid writing doesn't describe features — it describes a person in a situation, and then shows what changes.

Consider the difference between these two ways of describing FairShare:

**Abstract:**
> FairShare provides multi-touch attribution that ensures rep credit is maintained across the full customer journey.

**Specific:**
> She shared the link three weeks ago. Answered a question the same night. The customer went quiet — then bought last Tuesday through a Google search. She still got the credit. Because FairShare tracked the whole thing.

The first tells you what the feature does. The second tells you what it means to the person using it. Same capability — completely different impact.

Every feature has a version of this. Find the person. Find the moment. Find what changes when the system actually works.

---

### Name the problem before you name the solution.

Fluid content almost always starts with what's broken. Not to be negative — but because naming the real problem accurately is what makes a reader feel understood. If they feel understood, they'll listen to the solution.

The pattern is: **Here's what's actually happening → Here's why → Here's what we built.**

**Example from the Manifesto:**
> Attribution isn't a technical problem. It's a trust problem.
> Here's the real issue: reps don't trust that they'll get credit.
> So they don't share corporate content. They build their own bootleg versions. Or they just stop sharing altogether.
> Can you blame them?
> We built FairShare to fix this.

Notice: the problem is named, reframed (it's a *trust* problem, not a technical one), made human (can you blame them?), and *then* the solution is introduced. The solution lands harder because the problem was made real first.

**One important balance note:** The problem names the pain — but the writing should not live there. The goal is always to move through the problem toward the possibility on the other side. Direct selling companies have a genuine built-in advantage: a distribution channel of real people who believe in what they sell. That is something D2C brands spend millions trying to manufacture. Fluid exists to unlock that advantage — not just to describe what's broken. When the content earns it, let the upside breathe.

> Content fuels sharing. Sharing fuels traffic. Traffic fuels conversions. Conversions create new content. The flywheel spins — powered not by ads, but by people.

That is the other register of this voice. Problem-first does not mean problem-only.

---

### Say "you" and "we." Not "companies" and "platforms."

Fluid talks directly to the reader. The company being addressed is "you." Fluid is "we." This is not a whitepaper. It is a conversation.

**Wrong:**
> Direct selling companies often struggle with attribution systems that fail to properly credit distributors.

**Right:**
> Your reps aren't sharing because they've been burned. They sent someone to the website. Someone else got credit. They learned the system doesn't work for them. So they stopped.

---

### Write in present tense. Active voice. Always.

**Wrong:**
> Failed transactions have historically been a challenge for direct selling merchants.

**Right:**
> When a transaction fails in your world, it's not a stranger who shrugs and buys somewhere else. It's your rep's sister.

## What This Voice Is NOT

### Not hype. Not chest-thumping.

The Fluid voice does not say "industry-leading," "best-in-class," "revolutionary," or "game-changing." Those words are meaningless because every company uses them. If something is genuinely better, show why — don't assert it.

**Wrong:**
> Fluid's industry-leading attribution engine delivers best-in-class results for direct selling companies.

**Right:**
> If your rep planted the seed three weeks ago and the customer finally buys through a Google search, your rep still gets credit.

---

### Not a feature list. Not a bullet dump.

Features are not the story. What the feature means to a real person is the story. You can list capabilities in a knowledge base file — but when writing for marketing, always translate the capability into a human consequence.

**Wrong:**
> FairShare supports first touch, last touch, most active, sponsored ordering, orphan order rules, and enrollment credit attribution.

**Right:**
> FairShare tracks the whole journey. Not just who was last. Not just who owns the site. Whoever actually showed up.

---

### Not corporate. Not cautious.

The Fluid voice is willing to name what's broken — including by name. Shopify is named. The failure modes are described specifically. The voice does not hide behind vague language to avoid controversy.

> About half your transactions can't even happen on Shopify.
> That's not a typo.

That is a bold, specific, verifiable claim — stated directly. That is the Fluid voice.

---

### Not informal either.

The voice is human, not casual. It does not use slang, emoji-speak, or try-hard relatability. It is the voice of someone who has thought deeply about a problem and is sharing what they found — directly, without performance.

## Structural Patterns

### Short paragraphs. Often one or two sentences.

White space is not wasted space. Short paragraphs create rhythm. They make the reader keep moving. Long blocks of text signal "this is going to be work." Short blocks signal "stay with me."

### Use sentence fragments deliberately — not constantly.

Fragments create emphasis. They earn their place by slowing the reader down at exactly the right moment. Used too often, they become a tic. The writing starts to feel choppy rather than punchy.

The test: does the fragment land harder than a full sentence would? If yes, use it. If it just reads like an incomplete thought, finish it.

**Works:**
> When reps trust the system, they share. When they share, you get traffic.
> Simple as that.

> Shopify optimized for traffic.
> We-Commerce optimized for relationships.

"Simple as that" and "We-Commerce optimized for relationships" land because full sentences set them up. The fragment is the punctuation — not the whole paragraph.

### Repeat a word or phrase for effect.

The Manifesto does this well:

> We believe every transaction matters.
> We believe the rep who spent three months nurturing a relationship matters.
> We believe the syncs and the attribution and the checkout flow and the landing pages all matter — because they're all places where transactions go to die.

Repetition builds momentum. Use it when you want something to land.

### End sections with a landing statement.

The best Fluid paragraphs don't trail off — they end on something that sticks.

> When reps trust the system, they share. When they share, you get traffic.
> Simple as that.

> Powered not by ads. By people.

> One platform. Every transaction.
> Built for companies who refuse to treat their reps and customers as statistics.

## Words to Use

These words and phrases appear throughout strong Fluid content and reflect what the brand actually values:

- **people** — always preferred over "users," "distributors," "merchants"
- **trust** — the central emotional problem Fluid solves
- **credit** — what reps deserve and what the system should give them
- **effort** — what reps put in and what the system should recognize
- **share / sharing** — the core rep behavior the whole platform enables
- **journey** — how customers move toward a purchase (never "funnel" in brand voice)
- **relationship** — what commerce in direct selling is actually built on
- **the system** — used to describe the broken status quo that Fluid replaces
- **fix** — direct selling technology is broken; Fluid fixes it
- **confidence** — what reps need to share, what corporate needs to build

---

## Words to Avoid

These words are banned or heavily discouraged in Fluid marketing content:

| Avoid | Because |
|---|---|
| seamless | Everyone says this. It means nothing. |
| solutions | Vague corporate filler. Say what the thing actually does. |
| leverage | Corporate jargon. Use "use" or just say what you're doing. |
| robust | Says nothing specific. |
| empower | Overused to the point of meaninglessness. |
| utilize | "Use" works fine. |
| synergy | Never. |
| industry-leading | Unverifiable claim. Show, don't assert. |
| best-in-class | Same. |
| game-changing | Same. |
| revolutionary | Same. |
| innovative | Same. |
| ecosystem (overused) | Use sparingly. Don't use it just to sound technical. |
| users | Use "reps," "distributors," "customers," or "people." |
| merchants | Occasionally acceptable in technical contexts; avoid in brand voice. |
| platform (as a throwaway) | "Platform" means something. Don't use it as filler. |
| journey (as a buzzword) | "Customer journey" is overused in marketing. Use "path" or describe the actual sequence. |

## Audience-Specific Tone Adjustments

The core voice stays consistent. These are calibration notes for specific audiences.

### Writing for a CEO or Founder
Lean into vision and competitive stakes. They care about whether the technology is a liability or an advantage. Lead with what's possible, not what's fixed.

> Your technology should be a competitive advantage. Not the reason your best reps leave.

### Writing for VP of Sales / Field Leadership
Lead with rep trust and field outcomes. They've seen attribution break. They know what happens when reps lose faith. Speak to that directly.

> Your best reps aren't disengaged. They've been burned. FairShare fixes the underlying problem — not the symptom.

### Writing for COO / Operations
Lead with the cost of the current situation. Quantify where possible. They respond to specificity.

> How many vendors do you manage? How many integration failures did you troubleshoot last quarter? One platform eliminates that math entirely.

### Writing for a Rep (Direct)
Warmer, more personal. Less business-case framing. More "this is for you."

> You put in the work. You shared the link. You followed up. FairShare makes sure you get the credit — automatically, every time, without you having to fight for it.

### Writing Technical Explainers (Knowledge Base / Reference)
This is where the tone relaxes from brand voice into clear, informative prose. Still no jargon. Still present tense. Still direct. But more complete — less punchy, more precise. The knowledge base files in this project are an example of this register.

### Writing Social Visual Headlines (Text on the Graphic Itself)

This is a distinct writing challenge from captions or long-form content. A headline on a graphic has one job: stop the scroll. The reader hasn't committed to anything yet. They haven't read a caption, clicked a link, or decided to care. The visual text is what earns the next two seconds.

At this scale, most of the long-form principles still apply in spirit — but the execution is different.

**Keep it to five to eight words.** The headline should be readable in a glance. If someone has to work to parse it, they won't.

**Make it feel like it knows something they don't.** The best social headlines create a small gap — a tension between what the reader knows and what the headline implies. That gap is what generates the pause.

**Three formats that work for Fluid:**

*The reframe* — Takes something familiar and names it differently. Earns the pause because it feels slightly unexpected.
> Shopify is great. For 20% of your business.
> Reps share more when they trust they'll get credit.
> Attribution is the whole game. Fluid is how you win it.

*The direct statement of belief* — Confident, short, no qualification. Works best when the claim is specific enough to be interesting but broad enough to land without context.
> Every share should get credit.
> Reps don't leave companies. They leave broken systems.
> Commerce built on relationships. Not ad spend.
> The only e-commerce platform built specifically for direct selling.

*The named contrast* — Sets up a before/after or us/them in a single line. Efficient and concrete.
> Built for the sale. And the relationship behind it.
> One platform. Not fifteen.
> Purpose-built for direct selling. Not retrofitted for it.

**What to avoid on visuals:**

*Feature names without context* — "FairShare" or "Fluid Connect" as a standalone headline means nothing to someone who doesn't already know Fluid. Lead with what it does or what it solves, not what it's called.

*Questions that are too broad* — "Want to grow your business?" stops no one. Questions work when they're specific enough to feel like they're aimed at exactly one kind of person.

*Anything that could be from any company* — "Powerful tools for your team" could be Salesforce, Slack, or a software company you've never heard of. Fluid headlines should be ownable — they should feel like they could only come from a company that understands direct selling specifically.

**The insider test:** Would a direct selling executive who has never heard of Fluid read this headline and think "that's written for me"? If yes, you're in the right territory. If it could apply to any SaaS company or any industry, rewrite it.

## One-Sentence Summary of the Voice

Write like someone who has thought deeply about a broken industry, fixed a real part of it, and wants to tell you exactly what they found — without wasting your time.
