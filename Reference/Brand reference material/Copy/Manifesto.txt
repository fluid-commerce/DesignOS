Every Transaction Matters
A different way to think about direct selling technology

The conventional wisdom is wrong
Everyone tells direct selling companies to use Shopify. Or Stripe. Or Square. "That's what real e-commerce companies use," they say.
But here's the thing: you're not a real e-commerce company.
You're something different. Something those platforms weren't built for. And pretending otherwise is costing you more than you realize.

About half your transactions can't even happen on Shopify
That's not a typo. Roughly 50% of the transactions a direct selling company needs to process—subscriptions with commission structures, international orders with rep attribution, downline purchases—Shopify literally can't handle.
So why does everyone keep recommending it?
Because it's familiar. Because it's what they know. Because nobody stopped to ask whether "industry standard" actually fits your industry.

A 2% failure rate sounds small until you think about it
Traditional e-commerce treats failed transactions as statistics. Acceptable losses. Cost of doing business.
They can afford to think that way. They're selling to strangers. There's always another stranger.
You're not selling to strangers.
When a transaction fails in your world, it's not a stranger who shrugs and buys somewhere else. It's your rep's sister. Her best friend from college. The person she's been nurturing for three months.
That failed transaction doesn't just cost you a sale. It costs your rep her confidence. It teaches her that the system doesn't work. That her effort doesn't matter.
She'll share less next time. Maybe she'll stop sharing altogether.
One failed transaction. Ripples everywhere.

Higher risk means lower acceptance
Here's something the Shopify evangelists don't mention: your industry is classified as "higher risk" by payment processors.
That means your baseline decline rate is already worse than a typical online store. What's "normal" for them is catastrophic for you.
On a million dollars in monthly volume, you might be losing an extra $30,000-$50,000 to failed transactions. Every single month.
That's not a rounding error. That's a business problem hiding in plain sight.

We decided to care about every single one
Most platforms optimize for the average. They're fine with acceptable losses because they're playing a volume game.
We don't think that way.
We think about the mom who placed her first order at 11:47pm while nursing her baby. If her card declines and nobody tries again, she's not coming back. And her sister—your rep—just learned that sending people to your website is a gamble.
So we built a system that tries up to six times.
Six different merchant accounts. Six different routing options. Six chances to make sure that transaction actually happens.
Because for that mom, and for your rep, there is no "acceptable loss rate."

Friction is a transaction killer
Someone's ready to buy. They pull out their phone. The checkout wants them to create an account. They're at a red light. The moment passes.
You'll never know that transaction almost happened.
We eliminated the friction. Apple Pay. Google Pay. Buy Now, Pay Later. One click. No passwords. No forced account creation.
Every unnecessary step is a transaction at risk. So we removed the unnecessary steps.

Attribution isn't a technical problem. It's a trust problem.
Here's the real issue: reps don't trust that they'll get credit.
So they don't share corporate content. They build their own bootleg versions. Or they just stop sharing altogether.
Can you blame them? If you'd been burned before—sent a customer to the website only to watch someone else get credit—you'd be skeptical too.
We built Fair Share to fix this.
It doesn't just track the last click. It tracks the whole journey. First touch. Multiple touchpoints. Cross-device. Delayed purchases.
If your rep planted the seed three weeks ago and the customer finally buys through a Google search, your rep still gets credit.
When reps trust the system, they share. When they share, you get traffic.
Simple as that.

Your content is probably collecting dust
Marketing teams create beautiful assets that nobody uses.
Not because they're bad. Because reps don't see the point. Why share corporate content when you're not sure you'll get credit?
We made every piece of content shareable. Trackable. Attributed automatically.
Reps can add their face and their story. Customers see a human they trust. Everything connects.
Suddenly, your marketing budget actually does something.

Sync failures are silent killers
The order went through on the website but didn't sync to the commission engine. Or it synced with the wrong sponsor.
Now you're manually fixing records while reps lose faith that anything works the way it should.
We built one clean connection to your back office. Automatic sync. Real-time visibility. No mystery scripts that only one person understands.
When everything syncs correctly, nobody notices. That's the point.

You shouldn't need a developer to fix a landing page
Someone clicks through, ready to buy, and lands on a page that loads slowly and looks dated.
You know it's hurting conversions. But fixing it requires a developer, and they're busy, and it takes weeks to get anything done.
So the bad page stays up. Transactions leak out the bottom.
We built a drag-and-drop editor. Change the page yourself. Test what works. Launch in minutes instead of weeks.
Your landing pages should help you sell. If they're not, you should be able to fix them today.

Reps deserve real tools
Your reps are trying to run a business. Give them business tools.
Logged-in ordering. Subscription management. A CRM that shows who visited their links. Suggestions for when and how to follow up.
When reps can see what's happening and respond to it, more transactions happen.
This isn't complicated. It's just usually ignored.

We think differently about what matters

That game doesn't work for you.
In direct selling, every transaction carries compound potential. A new customer might become a rep. That rep might become a leader. That leader might build a team of hundreds.
It all starts with one transaction. The first one that works.
